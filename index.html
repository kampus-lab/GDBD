Creating Value
From Data

A Monetization Framework

Every organization should make more money from its data than it spends managing it. The instinct is to think that means selling data. It rarely does — there are two quieter, more common paths that get there first.

By Barbara H. Wixom, Cynthia M. Beath & Leslie Owens
One source, three routes to the bottom line
Why the word makes people nervous

"Data monetization" isn't a euphemism for selling data

Plenty of organizations — especially nonprofits and public agencies — flinch at the phrase, picturing shady data brokers. That reaction closes off two of the three real ways data turns into money, and they're the two most organizations are already halfway doing.

I

Improving

Use data to make existing work cheaper, faster, or less error-prone.

W

Wrapping

Use data to make a product worth more — so customers pay more, or buy more.

S

Selling

Trade an information solution directly for money, in cash or in kind.

Mixing up the playbooks is like calling a landscaper to fix your dishwasher. Each approach needs its own owner, its own tools, and its own way of proving the money actually showed up.

Strategy — Improving

Make the work itself cheaper

This is the oldest, most familiar route — descended from the 1990s business-process-reengineering movement. Data exposes where a process is slow or wasteful, and fixing it creates slack: extra time, lower cost, fewer errors.

The catch many teams miss: slack isn't money. It only becomes money once someone deliberately captures it — cutting a budget line, reassigning freed-up hours, shrinking a headcount. Measure the efficiency gain, then go claim it. Skip the second step, and the data hasn't been monetized at all; it's just made some employee's day a little easier.

Strategy — Wrapping

Make the product worth more

Wrapping bolts data-fuelled features onto something you already sell — a dashboard, a score, an alert, a recommendation — so customers value it more. "Product" is broad here: a good, a service, an elevator, even an insurance plan.

Realizing the value takes two checks: did customers actually notice (loyalty scores up, more enthusiastic referrals), and did the organization then cash that in — a higher price, more units sold, more related sales, or just slower erosion of a declining line. A wrap that delights customers but never reaches the price list hasn't monetized anything yet.

Strategy — Selling

Trade the data itself

The most literal route, and the one people default to first. An "information solution" is a standalone product built from data — sold for scarce insight, speed-to-market, clever algorithms, or just a better interface than the alternative.

It doesn't have to be cash. Retailers have swapped point-of-sale data for analytics reports since the late 1970s — that's still a financial transaction, and both sides have to keep deciding it's worth the exchange.

In closing

A loop that feeds itself

Engaging with data monetization — really tracking whether it's improving, wrapping, or selling its way onto the books — teaches an organization something it can reuse. Each round builds confidence and appetite for the next one: more data use leads to more value, which earns the budget and trust for still more data use. Multiply that across every team, and data monetization stops being a project and becomes a habit. That's the whole idea behind calling it everybody's business.

Field note

Banking you don't notice

Fintech has spent years promising consumers more control over their money — yet most banking apps still feel like a separate errand. One creative studio's view: stop thinking about payments as transactions, and start designing them as something that simply disappears into the rest of the experience.

frog's collaboration with J.P. Morgan explores wearables — rings, bracelets, necklaces — built for resorts, casinos, and entertainment venues, where guests want to pay without ever reaching for a phone. The thinking travels well beyond hospitality; healthcare, construction, and virtual goods are already circling the same idea.

01

Form follows emotion

Skip "form follows function." A payment object people actually want to wear has to connect emotionally first — something tangible to feel proud of, not just useful.

02

Solve for experience

Staying present has become the real luxury. The best version of invisible banking removes friction from a moment instead of interrupting it with a screen.

03

Predict the curve, then follow it

Co-build with partners, pilot with a small influential group — like a casino's highest-value guests — and let that group pull the idea toward the mainstream.

— Sean Rhodes, Executive Creative Director at frog, Adjunct Professor of Design Strategy, NYU Tandon School of Engineering
